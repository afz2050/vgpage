Cost of Living and Expenditure in Malaysia

By Afras Ashraf (33803218)

Over the past decade, Malaysians have faced steadily increasing living costs. From basic groceries to housing and transport, price changes affect every household — but not all regions or income groups experience these changes equally.

This visualisation explores how prices, inflation patterns, and household affordability differ across Malaysia using data from the Department of Statistics Malaysia (DOSM).

Price Changes Across Categories

Not all goods and services have risen in price at the same rate.

This grouped bar chart shows how CPI values for major item groups (such as Food, Housing, and Transport) have changed over recent years.

You can observe that Food and Non-Alcoholic Beverages consistently remain the largest contributor to inflation, followed by Housing, Utilities, and Transport.

Inflation Trends Over Time

The line chart below shows Malaysia’s CPI trend over time, representing the overall change in the cost of living.

Notice the periods where inflation rose sharply — around 2014–2015 (fuel subsidy removals), 2018 (GST to SST transition), and 2022–2023 (global price surges).

This steady upward trajectory reflects how the purchasing power of the Ringgit has gradually weakened over the years.

Urban vs Rural Differences

The cost of living gap between urban and rural areas is clearly visible in the CPI data.

While rural areas tend to have lower housing and service costs, urban residents face higher prices for transport, rent, and food.

The first line chart shows how CPI trends differ across strata over time.

The radar chart below compares CPI values for key goods (like food, housing, and transport) between urban and rural strata.

These charts highlight that urban households experience faster price increases, especially in transport and utilities.

Affordability Across States

While national CPI shows an overall increase, cost of living pressures vary widely across states.

The map below compares average household expenditure and income for each state, showing which regions face higher financial strain.

States like Kuala Lumpur, Selangor, and Penang show higher spending due to urban costs.

States such as Kelantan and Perlis show lower expenditures but also lower incomes.

Summary and Insights

Malaysia’s overall CPI has steadily increased from 2010 to 2025.

Food, housing, and transport remain the primary cost drivers.

Urban households experience higher inflation rates than rural ones.

Income growth has not kept pace with rising living costs, leading to affordability concerns in major cities.
